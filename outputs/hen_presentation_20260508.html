The path we took

The final result came from a sequence of very practical disappointments. Each one removed a tempting explanation and made the next design sharper.

1. Classic HEN worked, but it was too heavy 95.63%

The original 3-level HEN proved that the hierarchy made sense. The cost was brutal: many full backbones were stored for routers and experts. It was modular, but not economical.

2. Shared-backbone modular HEN saved parameters 94.67%

Sharing the feature extractor solved the storage problem, but the branch heads became too small relative to the backbone. It preserved modularity, yet the leaf experts were not expert enough.

3. Coarse-to-fine looked natural, then exposed the bottleneck 92.37%

A light router plus stronger downstream experts matched the intuition that top-level decisions need less detail. Top routing reached 99%+, but the leaf layer could not recover enough accuracy.

4. Food mid-level errors resisted obvious fixes 95.78% food mid

Higher resolution, wider mid heads, conservative crops, and attention crops all failed to move the food branch. That suggested the remaining errors were partly semantic and data-driven.

5. Compact Joint HEN crossed the line 95.63%

The best practical answer was a shared MobileNetV3-Large feature extractor with hierarchical probability heads. It kept the hierarchy, removed most duplicate compute, and crossed 95%.

Difficulties and fixes

The useful part of the project is not just the final model. It is also the map of what failed, because those failures explain why the final architecture is shaped the way it is.

Hard routing was brittle

Argmax routing meant an early mistake was unrecoverable. The joint model softened this by supervising all levels from the same shared feature representation.

Classic modularity was expensive

Independent routers and leaf experts duplicated whole CNN backbones. The compact HEN moved most capacity into one shared feature extractor.

Leaf experts needed real signal

Making branch heads tiny saved parameters, but underfit the hardest sibling classes. MobileNetV3-Large gave enough feature quality without returning to huge storage cost.

Food was not fixed by resolution

Food mid-level accuracy did not improve from 192px input, 512 hidden dimensions, or conservative crop settings. More pixels were not the missing ingredient.

Attention crop was too weak

Rule-based heatmap crops found salient regions, but not necessarily the labeled object. The ambiguity was often about which object should count.

Common-delta helped locally

Common versus difference features improved some hard branches such as aircraft and prepared_food, but did not beat the simpler compact joint model overall.

Resource story

At the 95% threshold, the comparison changes. Flat ConvNeXt-Tiny still wins on peak accuracy, but compact HEN wins on verified resource efficiency.

Final comparison

Flat ConvNeXt-Tiny reached 96.89% with 27.84M parameters and 4.456 GFLOPs. Compact Joint HEN reached 95.63% with 3.01M parameters and about 0.217 GFLOPs.

Why the last few points are hard

Some validation images mix multiple strong subjects. A bird with fruit, food beside another dominant object, or produce in a market scene can be reasonable for a human and still be counted wrong by the label protocol.

Final take

The hierarchy is worth keeping when it reduces resource cost without sacrificing the accuracy band that matters.

Flat CNN remains the strongest reference for peak accuracy. Compact Joint HEN is the stronger engineering answer for a verified 95%+ target under tight parameter and compute budgets.
